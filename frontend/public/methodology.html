Methodology

How the Fertility Forecaster model works, what data it uses, and what it doesn't account for.

Overview

Fertility Forecaster is a statistical simulation tool that estimates the probability of completing a desired family size given your age, health, reproductive history, and available fertility interventions.

How the Model Works

At a high level, the simulation runs a loop for each virtual couple:

  1. Each couple is assigned an individual fertility rate — some couples are naturally more fertile than others. On average, this starts at about a 23% chance of getting pregnant each cycle, but individual couples range anywhere from about 2% to 57% (see Section 8).
  2. Each menstrual cycle (~1 month), the model adjusts that couple's conception odds based on the woman's current age, BMI, and smoking status. Older women, women with higher BMI, and smokers have lower odds each cycle.
  3. A random draw determines whether conception occurs.
  4. If conception occurs, a second random draw determines whether the pregnancy results in a live birth or miscarriage, based on age-dependent miscarriage rates. This is further adjusted for any miscarriage history and male partner age, if applicable.
  5. If the couple fails to conceive naturally for a configurable number of consecutive cycles (default 12) and is open to IVF, the model uses frozen embryos first (if available), then frozen eggs (if available), then fresh IVF.
  6. The loop repeats until the couple achieves their desired number of children or the woman turns 45.

After simulating all 10,000 couples, the model reports what percentage achieved the desired family size.

Data Sources

1. Base Per-Cycle Conception Probability

Leridon, 2004 (via Habbema 2015)

The starting per-cycle chance of getting pregnant naturally, before any adjustments for age, BMI, or smoking. We set this at 23% for the youngest women in the model (ages 18–24). From there, it declines with age (see Section 2).

You may see ~25% cited elsewhere, but that figure tends to come from studies such as the PRESTO study that only recruited couples who were already trying to conceive, which naturally skews the number upward. The 23% figure is based on historical population data and is designed to represent the broader population, including couples who may take longer to conceive.

2. How Age Affects Your Odds

Wesselink et al., 2017

Your per-cycle odds of getting pregnant decline with age. The table below shows this as a ratio relative to the 21–24 age group, where the ratio is 1.00 (no decline). A ratio of 0.68 means your per-cycle odds have dropped to 68% of the baseline — so instead of 23%, you'd have about a 15.6% chance each cycle.

Why the difference? “Been pregnant before” includes any prior pregnancy — even ones that ended in miscarriage, not just live births. Women who have conceived at least once have demonstrated that their reproductive system can achieve pregnancy. Women who have never been pregnant include some who may have undiagnosed fertility issues (like blocked tubes or ovulation problems), which drags the group average down. This is a real selection effect, independently confirmed by two separate studies (Wesselink 2017 and Steiner & Jukic 2016). By the late 30s, this gap becomes very significant — a 35-year-old who has been pregnant before has about 22% odds per cycle, versus about 16% for someone who hasn't.

Note that male age does not meaningfully affect conception odds after controlling for female age — it only factors into miscarriage risk (see Section 4).

4. Male Age and Miscarriage Risk

Boxem et al., 2024

If the father is 40 or older, the risk of miscarriage roughly doubles (2×). The father's age does not affect the odds of getting pregnant — only whether the pregnancy carries to term. In the simulation, the male ages alongside the female, so this kicks in when he crosses 40.

6. BMI and IVF Success

Sermondade et al., 2019

Women with a BMI of 30 or higher have about a 15% lower chance of a live birth from IVF compared to normal-weight women. This applies to all IVF pathways (fresh IVF, frozen eggs, and frozen embryos).

8. Individual Variation Between Couples

van Eekelen et al., 2018

Not every couple has the same fertility. The 23% average is just that — an average. Some couples are naturally more fertile, and some less so, due to factors the model can't directly measure (like ovarian reserve, sperm quality, or uterine receptivity).

This matters a lot for predicting families with multiple children. If the model treated every couple as equally fertile, it would be too optimistic: it wouldn't account for the fact that a couple who struggled to conceive their first child will likely also struggle with their second.

To handle this, each simulated couple is randomly assigned a personal fertility rate at the start. That rate is fixed for life — it represents their inherent “fertility type.” The spread looks like this:

Couple percentilePer-cycle chance of conceiving
5th (least fertile)2.1%
25th9.2%
50th (median)19.3%
75th33.4%
95th (most fertile)56.7%

A couple at the 95th percentile has roughly a coin-flip chance of getting pregnant each month, while a couple at the 5th percentile faces long odds. Age-related decline is then applied on top of each couple's personal rate.

If you've already been trying: If you tell the model you've been trying for several months without success, it adjusts downward — the logic being that highly fertile couples would have already conceived by now, so you're more likely to be in the lower part of the distribution:

This doesn't mean your biology changed — it just means the model has a better guess about where you fall in the distribution.

Comparison with Habbema et al. 2015

Fertility Forecaster's model is based on the family completion modeling approach from Habbema et al. 2015, the standard reference for “when should couples start trying?” That study simulated 10,000 virtual couples cycle-by-cycle to calculate the latest age you can start trying and still have a given probability of completing your family:

Our model uses the same core simulation approach and matches Habbema's benchmark cutoff ages within ±1.5 years, but extends it in several ways:

Newer miscarriage data

Habbema used miscarriage rates from Leridon 2004, which were based on older data. We use Magnus et al. 2019, a registry study of 421,201 pregnancies in Norway with more precise age-specific rates. We also incorporate recurrent miscarriage risk (each consecutive loss increases the odds of the next one) and the effect of male partner age on miscarriage, which Habbema did not model.

Newer IVF data

Habbema's IVF success rates came from data available before 2015. IVF outcomes have improved meaningfully since then, particularly for older women, due to advances in embryo culture, vitrification, and lab protocols. Our model uses current per-transfer live birth rates from the CDC Assisted Reproductive Technology National Summary Reports and the UK HFEA dataset (250,000+ treatment records), which reflect these improvements.

Frozen eggs, frozen embryos, and PGT-A

Habbema's model included fresh IVF as the only assisted reproduction option. Ours adds frozen egg batches (with age-at-freeze-dependent per-oocyte rates from Hirsch 2024 and Namath 2025), frozen embryo batches (with higher per-transfer rates than fresh IVF), and PGT-A tested embryos (with euploid transfer rates from Jiang 2025). Users can enter their actual frozen inventory and the model will use it before falling back to fresh IVF.

Pregnancy history affects age-related decline

Habbema used a single age-decline curve for all women. We use two separate curves from Wesselink 2017: one for women who have never been pregnant, and one for women who have been pregnant before (see Section 2). The difference is large — at age 35, a woman who has been pregnant before retains about 96% of her baseline fertility, versus 68% for someone who hasn't. This isn't because pregnancy itself improves fertility; it's because women who have never conceived by their late 30s are more likely to include those with undiagnosed subfertility conditions, which drags the group average down.

In practice, this makes our model more optimistic for second and third children compared to Habbema. If you've already had one child, you've demonstrated that your reproductive system works, so the model applies the more favorable “been pregnant before” decline curve for your subsequent children.

BMI and smoking adjustments

Habbema's model did not account for BMI or smoking. Ours adjusts natural conception odds based on BMI (McKinnon 2016) and smoking status (Wesselink 2019), and applies a separate BMI penalty to IVF success rates (Sermondade 2019). The effects are modest below BMI 35 and for former smokers, but substantial at higher BMI or for regular smokers.

User-configurable parameters

Habbema's model hard-codes several assumptions:

  • Couples wait at least 15 months after the birth of a child before trying for the next pregnancy.
  • IVF begins after 2 years of unsuccessful natural trying if the woman is under 33, or 1 year if she is 33 or older.
  • Only 3 IVF cycles are attempted per child.

In our model, all of these are user-configurable — you can set the minimum spacing between births, how many months of natural trying before IVF, and how many total IVF cycles you're willing to attempt. This lets you model scenarios closer to your actual plans rather than relying on the study's assumptions.

Factors We Looked At But Didn't Include

  • Caffeine — No meaningful effect at normal consumption levels (Purdue-Smithe et al., 2022).
  • Alcohol — No clear effect at moderate intake (Høyer et al., 2020).
  • Marijuana — No overall effect for either partner (Wise et al., 2018).
  • Stress — Real effect (Schliep et al., 2019), but too variable to quantify as a stable input.
  • Vitamin D — No association with miscarriage risk (Subramanian et al., 2022).
  • Male BMI — No association with time to pregnancy on its own (Sundaram et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2022).
  • Intercourse frequency — Meaningful, but highly variable from month to month so difficult to quantify as a stable input.

Limitations

This model gives you population-level estimates. Your individual situation depends on many factors it can't measure, including ovarian reserve (AMH), specific conditions like PCOS or endometriosis, sperm quality, and genetic factors. The model assumes no underlying fertility diagnoses beyond what's captured by age, BMI, smoking, and reproductive history.

The data comes primarily from North American and Northern European populations. Results may not apply equally across all backgrounds.

This tool is not medical advice. Please consult a healthcare provider for a personalized fertility assessment.
